Two billion people work at the last mile — delivering medicines, distributing seeds, providing financial services in communities no platform has reached. We build the companies that make their work visible, manageable, and scalable.

What is a venture studio

Not a fund.
Not a consultancy.

A venture studio originates the ideas itself, builds the companies from scratch, and holds equity in what it creates.

Nate takes the founding risk. We source the problem, design the product, hire the first team, and provide the shared infrastructure — so each company starts with years of advantage rather than years of catch-up. In return, we hold 25–40% founding equity in every company we build.

The model was pioneered in deep-tech by firms like Flagship Pioneering — the studio behind Moderna. We are applying it to the last mile, where the problems are just as structural, and the compounding opportunity is just as large.

Why this model works here

Last-mile companies fail not because the market is too small, but because building them requires capabilities that no single founder can assemble alone — behavioural science, low-connectivity software architecture, field deployment experience, government relationships, and grant-to-equity financing expertise. Nate brings all of that to every company it builds. The studio is the unfair advantage.

"We're not here to consult on the problem. We're here to own the solution — and convert grant-funded problems into investable businesses."

Our product thesis

The last mile
needs four
kinds of
companies

Every sector that reaches the last mile is missing the same infrastructure. Not consumer apps. Not logistics platforms. The missing layer is always management, visibility, and distribution intelligence — and it is always sector-specific enough that no horizontal tool has cracked it.

Type 01
Intelligence & visibility
Managers, governments, and funders running last-mile programmes are making decisions with no data. The field is a black box. The first category of company Nate builds turns operational data — from field visits, task completions, and agent interactions — into dashboards and decisions. If you can't see it, you can't improve it.
Type 02
Workforce management
Recruiting, training, supervising, and incentivising field workers is done by hand in almost every programme on the continent. It is slow, expensive, and produces inconsistent results. The second category replaces that manual layer with software that works in low-connectivity environments, speaks to workers where they already are, and runs supervision at scale.
Type 03
Distribution infrastructure
Medicines, seeds, financial products, clean energy hardware — they all need to reach the same household. But they travel on separate trucks, through separate agents, managed by separate organisations. The third category builds the shared distribution layer that closes the last mile for multiple products at once, turning delivery into an economic asset for the agent doing it.
Type 04
Behavioural data networks
Every agent interaction contains a signal. What products are being asked for. Which households are adopting. Where demand is building before supply knows it. The fourth category captures that signal systematically, building data assets that are valuable to programme managers, to funders, and ultimately to the capital markets that price risk in emerging economies.
Our first two companies

Building from the
pattern we know best

We started where the evidence was strongest and the infrastructure gap most visible — agricultural agent networks and community health worker programmes. Both sectors are large, government-adjacent, and chronically underinvested in management infrastructure.

Agriculture · FMCG · Field Sales
AgentBI
AgentBI is a business intelligence platform for organisations that run field agent networks. It connects the functions that currently run in silos — recruitment, onboarding, task assignment, supervision, performance tracking, and incentives — and surfaces the resulting data as actionable insight for programme managers, corporate clients, ministries, and funders.

The field manager finally sees what is happening. The organisation finally knows who is performing, why, and under what conditions. AgentBI makes the invisible visible — without asking the field worker to change a single behaviour.

Backed by a $15M grant from the Gates Foundation; integrated with Apollo Agriculture in beta
Built on fieldwork from EMBE — one of the most rigorous studies of agricultural agent networks in Africa
Deployed via WhatsApp and low-connectivity interfaces agents already use — zero behaviour change required
Addressable across agriculture, FMCG, clean energy, and fintech field sales networks
In beta · Gates Foundation-backed
Community Health · Government
HealthBI
HealthBI is a managed service and intelligence platform for community health worker programmes run by governments and health ministries. Africa has committed to deploying 2 million community health workers — but the infrastructure to recruit, train, deploy, supervise, and retain them at that scale does not yet exist.

HealthBI provides it. It manages the full lifecycle of the CHW workforce — from recruitment to household visit data to performance payments — and gives programme managers the visibility they need to run a national health workforce like a high-functioning organisation rather than a collection of individuals.

Addresses Africa's $4.3B/year CHW workforce — fewer than 14% of workers currently salaried or formally supervised
Designed for government procurement: managed service contracts generate revenue from day one
Household visit data captured through HealthBI creates the continent's most granular community health dataset
CHW visit patterns are the earliest signal of disease burden — before any clinic or survey sees it
In development · Pipeline open
The studio process

From idea to
investable company

Most last-mile companies fail not because the problem is wrong, but because the company was underfunded, under-resourced, or built by a team without the right mix of skills. The studio model eliminates those failure modes by sharing infrastructure across every company it builds.

A
Ideation
Source 50 high-quality ideas
Each cycle begins with structured hypothesis generation — drawing on corporate leaders, development practitioners, field researchers, and domain experts to build a pipeline of "what if" questions about the last mile.
B
Validation
Stress-test down to 15
25 frameworks applied to every idea: customer validation, unit economics, field feasibility, killer experiments. The ones that survive this process have earned the right to be built.
C
Creation
Match EIRs, build MVPs
Each validated idea gets a dedicated Entrepreneur in Residence, a founding team, and a tight MVP runway. Real users, real data, clear kill conditions. No sacred cows.
D
Spin-out
One company earns its name
The company with measurable traction — a government contract, a paying client, a replicable deployment — spins out with a name, a full team, and Nate's 25–40% equity stake. The cycle repeats.
Capital structure

Built for public good,
designed for private scale

Nate sits at the intersection of philanthropic capital and private markets — and that intersection is intentional. The earliest validation is funded by grants. The growth stage is funded by strategic corporates. The exit is driven by the private market. Each type of capital does what it is best at.

Grants
De-risks ideation and early validation. Gates Foundation, CGIAR, bilateral aid. Funds the work that is too early for investment but too important to leave unfunded.
Studio
Builds and validates product-market fit. Nate's own capital covers EIR costs, shared infrastructure, and the operating layer every portfolio company benefits from.
Strategic
Enters at growth stage. Mastercard, Google, Unilever, Microsoft — corporates with distribution reach and acquisition intent who cannot originate these opportunities themselves.
Markets
Prices the exits. Secondary sales at Series A and full exit at Series B. By that point, the company has converted from grant-funded to investable — and the market knows what to do with it.
