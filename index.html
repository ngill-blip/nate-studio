Public service products

Trillions of dollars flow into public services each year. The products managing them — hiring, training, supervision, decision-making — are almost universally grant-dependent, non-scalable, and invisible to private capital. Nate builds the infrastructure to change that.

The Thesis
Public sector problems are not inherently unbankable. They fail commercially because no one has built them with the right architecture — country-first in design, multi-country in scale, convertible from grant to investment.

The founding insight

A structure problem,
not a demand problem

Every country that delivers public services depends on a ground-level workforce to reach its population. Hundreds of billions flow into these systems every year. The demand is not in question.

The problem is structural. These products are built for grant cycles, not for scale. They are built by governments for one country, by donors for one programme, by NGOs for one context. They are built to be funded, not to be investable.

"The market failure in public sector products is not that there is no market. It is that no one has built the products in a way that a private investor can enter."

Private conglomerates are already moving in — Veritas Capital, EQT, TA Associates, and PSG are deploying billions into govtech. NEOGOV was acquired for $3B+ in 2025. The public sector is being commercialised. The question is whether products built for emerging markets and frontline workforces get built to participate in that transition — or get left behind.

Nate's answer: build every product from day one with dual architecture — grant-fundable today, investor-ready tomorrow. That design is the product.

The Nate model — how we build differently
Thematic funds, not a portfolio scatter
Each fund launches around a single public sector theme. Every company is a thesis test. Portfolio companies share infrastructure, data, and market relationships. The fund compounds; the companies don't start from zero.
Dual architecture: grant-ready and investor-ready
Every product is designed with two funders in mind from day one — the philanthropic capital that de-risks early validation, and the private capital that enters when unit economics are proven. Multi-country architecture, fee-generating from first deployment, exit-legible from the first contract.
The conversion is the moat
A consulting firm can't convert a grant-funded programme into an investable company — it has no equity stake. A pure VC fund can't de-risk the early validation — it has no grant access. Nate sits at the intersection of both. Converting grant businesses to investable ones is the product, not a by-product.

The investment model

A different capital stack
for a different kind of product

Public sector products fail commercially for a predictable set of reasons: built for single-country deployment, locked to grant cycles, designed by programme logic not product logic, and exiting via government handover rather than M&A.

Why the current model fails

GovTech in developed markets has cracked the procurement model. But frontier markets remain grant-dependent by default. Procurement is fragmented, standards are non-existent, and products are built bespoke for each context. No one captures the value of having done it before.

Why Nate's model is different

Nate's capital structure matches the actual risk profile of each stage. Philanthropic capital covers ideation and early validation. Nate studio capital builds the product and finds PMF. Strategic corporates and growth PE enter when unit economics are proven. Each fund has a clear path from grant to investable — built into the product from day one.

The investment arc

From grant-funded
to investor-ready

Phase 1 · Grant Entry
1
Enter with philanthropic capital, validate with field reality
Use existing grant relationships — Gates, multilaterals, development funds — to enter the problem space with zero commercial risk. Build field evidence. Run real deployments. Most attempts die here because the product was built for the report, not for the user.
Phase 2 · Product Architecture
2
Build with dual architecture — not a pivot, a design choice
Every product Nate builds is architected for two futures simultaneously: continued grant funding for the programme logic, and private investment for the product logic. Multi-country by design, fee-generating from first deployment, exit-legible from the first contract.
Phase 3 · Private Entry
3
Unit economics proven, strategic investors enter
Once a company demonstrates repeatable deployment, fee revenue, and multi-country replication, private capital has a legible entry point. Strategics enter as investors or acquirers. Growth PE enters for scale. Nate's equity — built at near-zero cost during the grant phase — is now worth multiples.
The structural moat

The relationships and data
private capital can't buy

The public sector is not accessible to private capital by default. Government contracts, ministerial relationships, and philanthropic grant pipelines take years to build. They cannot be replicated by a VC fund writing a cheque. Nate's grant relationships — Gates Foundation, CGIAR, multilateral development banks — are the entry point that no competitor can shortcut.

The data moat builds on top of that. Every deployment generates proprietary intelligence on what works in field workforce management. By the time a competitor arrives, Nate has the contracts, the data, and the track record that takes a decade to build.
