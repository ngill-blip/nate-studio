The founding insight

A legibility problem,
not a motivation problem

Every company that reaches a rural or underserved market depends on a ground-level worker — a community health worker, an agrodealer, a sales rep, a fintech field officer. These are the people who physically close the last mile. They are the operating layer that no technology has or can replace, and no logistics algorithm has or can make obsolete.

And yet the systems managing them are almost universally a patchwork of paper forms, messaging groups, Excel trackers, and supervisors who cannot see what is happening in the field. In FMCG alone, 86% of sales in emerging markets flow through informal channels invisible to the brand. A company running 500 field reps across a country sets targets based on what leaves the warehouse, not what reaches the shelf.

"Why does every company that sells, distributes, or delivers at the last mile manage the people doing it the same broken way?"

The problem is not motivation or training. It is that every function touching ground-level workers — recruiting, onboarding, supervision, incentives, coaching — runs in its own silo, with its own data and its own blind spots. Field performance is ungovernable not because it is unknowable, but because no one has built the infrastructure to manage it.

That infrastructure is AgentOS. It connects the functions, surfaces the data, and runs invisibly underneath the interfaces field workers already use. The field worker sees the interface they already use. The organisation finally sees what is happening.

The operating layer
AgentOS handles everything every last-mile company needs but should never have to rebuild — recruiting, credentialing, training, task assignment, supervision, incentives, performance tracking. It gets smarter with every Nate deployment. A CHW programme in Kenya teaches it something a seed dealer programme in India immediately benefits from. The knowledge compounds across the entire portfolio.
Invisible infrastructure, familiar front-end
The field worker never sees AgentOS. They see WhatsApp — or whatever interface they already use. AgentOS runs silently underneath, turning natural conversations into verified task completions, performance data, and supervision signals. Adoption is not a problem you solve. It disappears. You are not asking anyone to change behaviour. You meet them exactly where they already are.
The intelligence exchange
Every deployment generates two types of intelligence — agent intelligence (who performs, how, under what conditions) and domain intelligence (what works in seeds, in health, in FMCG). Nate sits at the intersection of all of it. A company that wants to work with farmer agents gets access to Nate's accumulated knowledge of what makes a great one. In return, their deployment feeds domain intelligence back into the network. Both sides get smarter. The network grows more valuable with every sector it touches.

Our ongoing hypotheses
Can the agent become an early warning system?
The earliest signal of any disease outbreak is not a clinic or a survey — it is the person who visits households before anyone else does. What if the infrastructure managing that visit also captured what she saw?
Can one worker do many things?
A health worker, a seed advisor, a financial agent — governments and companies train them separately, deploy them separately, and manage them separately. But they visit the same households. What if a single worker, properly supported, could cross skill boundaries — and what if AgentOS was the infrastructure that made that manageable?
Can the agent replace the supply chain?
Contraceptives, seeds, and medicines travel on separate trucks to the same village. What if the agent was the distribution point?
Can the agent make markets visible?
Prices, demand, stock levels at the last mile are invisible to everyone above the distribution chain. What if every agent visit was a market data point?
The investment arc

From hypothesis
to company to scale

Phase 1 · Now
1
Build the studio, crowdsource the hypotheses
Set up operations across London and Nairobi. Run structured hypothesis generation — drawing on practitioners, field programmes, and domain experts — to build a pipeline of 50+ "what if" questions about the last mile. Begin validating AgentOS in its first anchor deployment.
Phase 2 · +12 months
2
From 50 hypotheses to 2 proto-companies
Run each hypothesis through a disciplined kill process — customer validation, field testing, killer experiment. Most die. Two survive with enough evidence to warrant building. Each becomes a numbered proto-company: no name, tight runway, clear kill conditions.

3
From 2 proto-companies to 1 spun-out company
The two proto-companies compete on evidence. One proves measurable traction — a government contract, a paying corporate client, a replicable deployment. That company earns a name, a founding team, and Nate's 25–40% equity stake. It spins out. The process repeats.
The structural moat

Behavioural data,
not software

Software can be copied. Behavioural data cannot. Every deployment Nate runs generates knowledge that no competitor can replicate without running the same deployments — which supervision models hold across cultures, which incentive structures reduce attrition, which interventions produce outcomes that funders will actually pay for.

But the deeper moat is not data. It is trust. Governments and multinationals do not hand over their last-mile workforces to organisations without a track record. Nate builds that track record systematically — one deployment, one sector, one country at a time. By the time a competitor arrives, Nate has the contracts, the data, and the relationships that take a decade to build.
